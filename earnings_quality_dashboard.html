How the Earnings Quality Score is calculated.

Each company is scored on two signals:

Cash Conversion Ratio (CCR): Operating Cash Flow divided by Net Income. Measures how much of every dollar of reported profit actually became cash. A ratio close to or above 1.0 indicates high earnings quality. A ratio significantly below 1.0 suggests reported profits are not being realized in cash, which can signal aggressive revenue recognition or understated expenses.

Accrual Ratio: Net Income minus Operating Cash Flow minus Investing Cash Flow, divided by Total Assets. Measures how much of a company's earnings are driven by non-cash accounting entries rather than actual cash generation. A lower or negative accrual ratio is better; it means the company's cash performance exceeds what accounting rules require it to report.

Both signals are normalized to a 0-1 scale using Min-Max scaling so they contribute equally to the final score. The composite Earnings Quality Score is their simple average. A score closer to 1.0 indicates higher earnings reliability. Only companies with positive net income are included here. Why these companies are scored differently.
The Cash Conversion Ratio (CCR) becomes unreliable when net income is negative. A company posting high net income but low operating cash flow signals poor earnings quality, but the inverse does not hold when net income itself is negative. For these 27 companies, we evaluate cash trajectory instead.

Cash Position: Total cash and cash equivalents on the balance sheet. Indicates how much liquidity the company has available.

Operating Cash Flow (OCF): Actual cash generated or consumed by core business operations. A company can report a net loss but still generate positive OCF. This often occurs because losses are driven by non-cash charges like depreciation or impairment rather than actual cash outflows. Shown in green if positive, red if negative.

OCF / Cash Ratio: Operating cash flow relative to cash reserves. A ratio above 1.0 means the company is generating more cash than it currently holds. A negative ratio means cash is being consumed.

Cash Flow Trend: Reflects both short-term (year-over-year) and long-term (3-year) direction simultaneously:
  • Consistently Improving: OCF is better than both last year and 3 years ago
  • Recently Improving: OCF is better than last year but worse than 3 years ago; recovery signal
  • Recently Deteriorating: OCF is worse than last year but better than 3 years ago; recent setback on otherwise improving trajectory
  • Consistently Deteriorating: OCF is worse than both last year and 3 years ago; sustained decline
